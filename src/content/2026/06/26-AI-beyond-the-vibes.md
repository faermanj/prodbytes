# Enterprise Decision Management: The Humans in the Loop

Agents are everywhere these days, including in the enterprise, but it's not only for the better. From security vulnerabilities to production outages, we see AI playing a significant role both in preventing AND causing those issues. The claims of success and the hype on social media greatly exaggerate what can be expected in an enterprise context, where technology needs to be secure, reliable, and accountable. In this article, let's discuss how corporate AI adoption can be balanced between the skepticism and the hype.

## Modern Application Integration

Traditionally, application integration in the enterprise revolved around mostly two elements: forms and reports. A lot of them. Entire suites of products were built to help developers build them. However, that approach created the famous monolithic "silos": concentrated masses of business data and logic that are hard to change and integrate. Then we shifted to service integration instead, making sure that business data and logic are managed cohesively, with flexible integration adapters. That empowers organizations to go beyond a single user interface for each business domain, allowing us to build the diverse perspectives required by the modern marketplace.

That is dramatically changing with agents and chat, not only replacing the forms, but forcing businesses to be available outside the walls of their web or mobile applications. We'll soon be able to book an entire trip, or get the best insurance we need, without going through all the alternatives or delegating to a broker that adds no further value. Now that natural language integration is largely solved, all of that can be reduced to a single prompt or a simple specification.

That brings huge pressure to the provider side of the transaction. Businesses need not only to provide such integration capabilities, but to make sure that they follow the same rules and processes. When the decisions are subject to an audit, internal or regulatory, the interface does not matter. The organization needs to demonstrate precisely why and how the decision was made, be it the price of a policy or the treatment of a patient.

## Decision Modeling and Management

Such business decisions are often complex, and decomposed into many "microservices", making them difficult to audit. They often require collecting and correlating data from each system, with different backends and capabilities. This brings back the need for a centralized component, to enforce compliance and accountability. However, how can we add that hub without bringing back the monolithic silo?

Decision management systems are a big part of that answer. By modeling rules and processes in visual and executable formats, such as [DMN](https://docs.aletyx.ai/guides/examples/dmn/dmn-intro/) and [BPMN](https://docs.aletyx.ai/guides/examples/bpmn-example/), organizations can ensure consistency, adapt quickly to change, and preserve auditability. With the model at the core of the system, it does not matter how many services were used to bring in the data or how the transaction was placed. Organizations can modernize infrastructure and software independently, without losing track of business logic, keeping every decision explainable.

That may sound complicated, but here is another opportunity for using AI agents. Business models are visual representations, but under the hood, they are just code in text format. So in the same way that an LLM can create a Python function, it can turn a policy into a correct business model, or change the model to adapt to new requirements and regulations. For the agent, it is just another language. That significantly reduces the learning curve, and makes business modeling a tool as effective for analysts and managers as it has been for developers. But that brings another question: if AI is on both sides of the transaction, where do the humans fit in?

## The Humans in the Loop

Agents provide extremely valuable capabilities to organizations, including language processing and comprehension, but "intelligence" is much deeper and more nuanced than technology can handle. Open-Source Software is one area that makes this point quite evident. Open-source has been a better way to build for decades now, but AI brings valid and hard challenges to its management. There are even [companies trying to kill it](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=bpqFZBWcStU). However, so much of the world's infrastructure is built upon Open-Source that it's clear it's not going away anytime soon. But it does need to change.

In its origin, Open-Source Software was built by rebellious individuals, fighting for the freedom to own and fix their applications, without requiring corporate permission and licensing agreements. Individuals still play a role in Open-Source development; however, it is no longer just a hobby or occasional contribution. Companies are now organized into foundations that maintain large projects, built by professionals, stewarding a huge shared property of code and knowledge. Even for offering commercial products, it is far more effective and safe for both providers and consumers to leverage the Open-Source Software model. Nowadays, most successful software offerings are built and marketed in this integrated model, where companies have both the freedom to change and the possibility of being supported.

In our case, for decision modeling and management, you'll find these people who have been building for decades in the open-source community of [Apache KIE](https://kie.apache.org/), employed by the companies that support it, such as [IBM](https://www.ibm.com/es-es/products/business-automation-manager-open-editions/process-automation-manager), [Aletyx](https://aletyx.ai), and other members of the [Decision Management Community](https://dmcommunity.org/).
